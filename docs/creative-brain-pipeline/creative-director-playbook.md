# Creative Director Playbook

**Status:** Permanent — the operating manual of the Creative Director Brain
**Nature:** Not a knowledge base of lessons (see `creative-director-
knowledge.md`) and not a catalogue of failures (see `creative-director-
failure-library.md`). This is the decision-making process itself — how a
world-class Creative Director thinks through every commercial and creative
choice on an overseas-recruitment advertisement, before any pixel is
rendered. No code, no prompts, no implementation, no architecture.

Each decision below: **Decision** · **Inputs considered** · **Trade-offs**
· **Why one option wins** · **Candidate psychology** · **Commercial
reasoning** · **Creative reasoning** · **Examples** · **Permanent rule**.

---

## 1. Opportunity ranking

**Decision:** Of everything true about this job (country, salary, industry,
project, employer, positions, benefits, interview, trust), which single
fact does the ad lead with?

**Inputs considered:** Destination country and its prestige tier; presence
and size of a salary figure; industry attractiveness; whether the project
is a mega-project/shutdown; whether the employer is a recognizable
"magnet" brand; headcount; grounded benefits; interview urgency; agency
trust signals.

**Trade-offs:** Leading with the employer feels natural (it's often the
first field in the source data) but wastes the lead position if the brand
isn't recognizable. Leading with salary is powerful but only if a real
figure exists — an ad can't lead with money it doesn't have. Leading with
country works when the destination itself carries prestige (Saudi Arabia,
UAE) but is a weaker lead for a less differentiated destination.

**Why one option wins:** The lever with the strongest *grounded, verifiable
pull for this specific candidate audience* wins — never the lever that
happens to be listed first in the source data.

**Candidate psychology:** A scrolling candidate gives an ad about one
second of attention. They are asking one question first: "is this worth
stopping for me, specifically?" The lead claim must answer that in the
first glance.

**Commercial reasoning:** The highest-value real estate on the canvas
(headline position, largest type) must be spent on whichever fact
actually converts attention into a read, not on whichever fact was easiest
to place there.

**Creative reasoning:** A ranked hierarchy of levers keeps every ad
coherent — one clear message, supported by everything else, rather than a
grab-bag of equally loud claims.

**Examples:** Halliburton (a genuinely recognizable brand) can lead with
the employer name. Bilfinger (credible, not a magnet) cannot — it leads
with the Saudi Arabia opportunity and shutdown-project scale instead, with
Bilfinger demoted to a trust line.

**Permanent rule:** Rank the levers (Country → Salary/Earning → Industry →
Project → Employer → Positions → Benefits → Interview → Trust) and commit
to exactly one hero — the rank order is a default priority, but any lever
can win if its grounded strength for this campaign clearly exceeds the
others.

## 2. Salary vs. country vs. employer priority

**Decision:** When more than one strong lever is available, which of these
three specifically takes the headline?

**Inputs considered:** Whether a real salary figure is grounded in the
source; whether the destination country is itself a prestige signal
(Saudi Arabia, UAE) vs. a stable-but-unremarkable one; whether the
employer is a magnet brand (instantly recognized) or merely credible.

**Trade-offs:** Salary is the single most universally motivating lever
when present — but many requirements simply don't carry a disclosed
figure, and inventing one is never an option. Country prestige is always
available (every job has a destination) but is a softer motivator than a
concrete number. Employer name is the strongest possible lead when it's a
true magnet brand, but actively weak when it isn't.

**Why one option wins:** Salary, when grounded, generally outranks country,
which generally outranks a merely-credible employer name. A magnet
employer can outrank all three, because brand recognition alone can
trigger the "worth stopping for" response as fast as a number can.

**Candidate psychology:** Money answers "what do I get." Country answers
"is this a place I want to go." Employer answers "can I trust this is
real and reputable." All three matter, but only one should visually lead
at a time — the others still appear, at lower prominence, in the body.

**Commercial reasoning:** Grounded salary consistently produces the
strongest scroll-stop and click-through in this category; it is
prioritized whenever it's actually present. In its absence, the next
strongest available truth takes over.

**Creative reasoning:** Consistency in priority order lets the whole ad
system feel deliberate rather than arbitrary — a candidate who sees
multiple KAI ads learns to expect the strongest fact up top every time.

**Examples:** Bilfinger has a real salary figure → salary/earning
motivation leads. Halliburton has no disclosed salary but a magnet brand
→ the brand and the Saudi Arabia opportunity lead instead. Royal Palace
Hotel has a modest but present salary and a credible-but-unrecognized
employer → salary leads over the employer name.

**Permanent rule:** Salary (when grounded) > country prestige > credible
(non-magnet) employer name, in headline priority — except that a genuine
magnet-brand employer can outrank all three.

## 3. Hero selection (who or what is the visual subject)

**Decision:** What appears as the dominant visual subject of the canvas —
a worker, a team, a location/environment, or no human figure at all?

**Inputs considered:** Industry (oil & gas → refinery/worker; hospitality
→ interior/staff; healthcare → clinical setting); headcount (single-role
vs. mass hiring); whether facial closeness would misrepresent an
individual not actually hired yet; the emotional tone required (money,
prestige, urgency, career).

**Trade-offs:** A single worker shown small against a vast environment
communicates scale and aspiration but can feel impersonal for a
close-knit or hospitality-driven role. A team shot communicates
camaraderie and mass-hiring energy but dilutes individual aspiration. A
pure environment shot (no human) foregrounds the place/project but loses
the human "picture yourself here" pull entirely.

**Why one option wins:** The hero is chosen to match the actual emotional
lead of the ad, not the industry stereotype on autopilot — a mega-project
shutdown wants the lone worker dwarfed by structure (scale = opportunity);
a walk-in hospitality drive wants a small group in motion (activity =
opportunity now).

**Candidate psychology:** Humans respond most strongly to human figures
they can imagine themselves as — but only if the figure is aspirational
and anonymous (never posed for camera, never a specific identifiable
face), so every viewer can project themselves into the role.

**Commercial reasoning:** The hero occupies the single largest, most
expensive piece of visual real estate; it must justify that space by
directly reinforcing the ad's dominant emotional hook, not by defaulting
to "a worker in a hard hat" regardless of context.

**Creative reasoning:** One hero, one story — mixing hero types (a worker
here, a building there, a team somewhere else) fragments the visual
narrative the same way mixing headline claims fragments the message.

**Examples:** Oil & gas mega-project → a lone worker as a small figure
against refinery structures. Mass hiring / walk-in drive → a small group
in motion. Premium hospitality → clean, refined interior with staff
present but not dominant.

**Permanent rule:** Exactly one hero subject, chosen to match the ad's
dominant emotional hook and industry reality — never generic, never mixed,
never an identifiable individual face.

## 4. Candidate hook creation

**Decision:** What is the one sentence-equivalent idea the headline must
communicate?

**Inputs considered:** The winning lever from Opportunity Ranking (§1);
the emotional tone it implies (money, prestige, urgency, career growth,
stability); the audience's likely prior exposure to similar ads (is this
hook differentiated or generic).

**Trade-offs:** A specific, concrete hook ("SR salary + food + OT" or
"Saudi Aramco Mega Project") is more persuasive but requires strong
grounded facts to support it. A generic hook ("URGENT HIRING," "Great
Opportunity") is always available regardless of what facts exist, but
persuades nobody because every competing ad says the same thing.

**Why one option wins:** Specificity always wins over genericness when the
underlying fact is strong enough to support it; a generic device is only
acceptable as a last resort when no differentiated fact exists at all,
and even then it must still reflect a real, grounded urgency signal
(a real interview date), not decoration.

**Candidate psychology:** Generic claims are invisible — candidates have
seen "URGENT HIRING" hundreds of times and it has stopped registering.
Specific claims (a number, a name, a place) interrupt the scroll because
they read as real information, not marketing noise.

**Commercial reasoning:** The hook is what determines whether the ad
converts a scroll into a read; a generic hook caps the ad's performance
regardless of how strong the rest of the execution is.

**Creative reasoning:** The hook and the hero (§3) must reinforce the same
idea — a money hook pairs with an environment/scale hero, an urgency hook
pairs with an in-motion team hero.

**Examples:** "Earning — salary opportunity" (Bilfinger, grounded figure).
"Saudi Arabia · Oil & Gas" (Halliburton, no salary, so destination +
sector leads). A bare "URGENT HIRING" banner with nothing behind it is the
failure case, not the standard.

**Permanent rule:** The hook must be the single strongest grounded,
specific claim available — a generic urgency device is only permitted
when tied to a real, date-bound signal, never as a default.

## 5. Emotional direction

**Decision:** Which emotional register does the entire ad commit to —
money, opportunity/career, prestige, urgency, or stability?

**Inputs considered:** The winning opportunity lever; country-specific
emotional tone (see §17); industry attractiveness; urgency signals
(interview dates, spot selection); whether the offer reads as career-
building or as immediate income.

**Trade-offs:** Money-led emotion is the most universally strong pull but
can feel transactional for premium/executive-tier roles. Prestige-led
emotion elevates perceived quality but underperforms for mass-hiring,
income-motivated audiences. Urgency-led emotion drives fast action but,
overused, reads as pressure rather than opportunity.

**Why one option wins:** The emotional register must match the winning
lever from §1/§2 — an ad that leads with salary but visually communicates
"prestige" (or vice versa) feels internally inconsistent and undermines
trust.

**Candidate psychology:** Emotion, not fact density, is what makes a
candidate stop scrolling in the first second; the facts (which follow) are
what make them believe it once stopped.

**Commercial reasoning:** A single, committed emotional register performs
better than a blended one — mixed signals read as unfocused marketing,
which this audience has learned to scroll past.

**Creative reasoning:** Every downstream decision — colour grading (§9),
lighting, hero pose, typography weight — flows from the emotional
register chosen here; it is the root decision the rest of the visual
system depends on.

**Examples:** Bilfinger (grounded salary + shutdown urgency) → money +
urgency. Saudi Aramco (magnet brand + mega-project) → opportunity +
prestige. Royal Palace Hotel (walk-in drive) → urgency, moderated by a
premium hospitality tone.

**Permanent rule:** Choose exactly one dominant emotional register per ad,
derived directly from the winning opportunity lever — never blend
registers, and never default to a register the grounded facts don't
support.

## 6. Visual storytelling

**Decision:** What single scene/environment does the background depict?

**Inputs considered:** Industry (oil & gas, construction, hospitality,
healthcare, aviation, manufacturing, etc.); whether the project is a
mega-project/shutdown (implies scale); destination country's atmospheric
character (Gulf desert light, etc.).

**Trade-offs:** A highly specific, industry-accurate environment
(a refinery, a shipyard, a luxury hotel interior) is the most credible and
differentiated choice but requires confidence the industry classification
is correct. A generic "professional facility" fallback is always safe but
communicates nothing distinctive.

**Why one option wins:** The most specific environment that can be
confidently and correctly matched to the grounded industry wins; a
generic fallback is only used when no specific match applies — it is
never allowed to contradict the actual industry (see the Hospitality/
Hospital and Refined/Refinery failures in the failure library).

**Candidate psychology:** A candidate recognizes their own trade in an
accurate environment within a glance; an inaccurate or generic one creates
a moment of doubt ("is this really relevant to me?") that a scrolling
viewer won't pause to resolve.

**Commercial reasoning:** Visual accuracy to industry is itself a trust
signal — it demonstrates the ad was made specifically for this
opportunity, not templated.

**Creative reasoning:** One story, matching one hero (§3) and one
emotional register (§5) — visual storytelling is never treated as
independent decoration.

**Examples:** Oil & gas → refinery at golden hour. Construction →
cranes and rising structures. Hospitality/hotel → refined luxury
interior (never a hospital interior, never an oil refinery, however the
industry text happens to be worded).

**Permanent rule:** The background scene must be the most specific
environment that genuinely, verifiably matches the grounded industry —
generic fallback only when no specific match exists, and never a scene
that contradicts the industry.

## 7. Typography hierarchy

**Decision:** How is emphasis distributed across company name, headline,
positions, salary, and fine print?

**Inputs considered:** What's leading the ad (§1/§2); how many distinct
information types must coexist (positions, salary, benefits, interview,
trust); print/screen legibility at both full size and mobile-feed scale.

**Trade-offs:** More font variety can feel expressive but reads as
inconsistent across a multi-campaign publisher; a rigidly small role set
is simpler to apply consistently but must be flexible enough to serve
every campaign's needs (a dense 129-position poster vs. a single-role
box).

**Why one option wins:** A small, fixed set of roles — mapped to meaning,
not to decoration — wins because it reproduces a trustworthy, print-shop-
grade hierarchy every time, regardless of content volume.

**Candidate psychology:** Readers scan headlines before they read body
text; a hierarchy that doesn't clearly separate "the one thing that
matters" from "the supporting detail" forces the reader to do the sorting
work themselves, and most won't.

**Commercial reasoning:** Consistent hierarchy across every campaign
builds recognition of KAI-produced ads as a category of reliably
well-organized information, independent of which agency or employer is
featured.

**Creative reasoning:** Five roles (hero/company name, section bars,
dense position lists, salary/key emphasis, address/fine print) is enough
expressive range to serve every archetype without becoming arbitrary.

**Examples:** A heavy display face for "BILFINGER" or "HALLIBURTON"; a
bold condensed face for section bars; a black/heavy face reserved for
salary numerals so money is instantly locatable by weight alone.

**Permanent rule:** Every text element maps to exactly one of five fixed
typographic roles; the role, not the campaign, determines the treatment.

## 8. Colour psychology

**Decision:** What colour palette and grading carries the ad's emotional
register?

**Inputs considered:** Destination country's premium colour association
(§17); emotional register (§5); whether the variant is B/W or Colour DTP;
industry mood (warm/industrial vs. clean/corporate).

**Trade-offs:** A warm, saturated palette (desert gold, amber) reads as
premium and aspirational but can feel garish if pushed to raw primary
tones. A cool, corporate palette (deep blue, clean neutrals) reads as
trustworthy and professional but can feel cold for opportunity-led,
aspirational hooks. Pure B/W removes the choice entirely and relies on
contrast and typography alone.

**Why one option wins:** Colour is chosen from a curated, destination-
and-mood-aware palette, never a raw default — the winning palette is
whichever curated option most reinforces the chosen emotional register
without introducing a competing colour temperature.

**Candidate psychology:** Colour is processed before text; a palette that
reads as premium or urgent (correctly matched to the ad's actual content)
primes the candidate's emotional response before they've read a single
fact.

**Commercial reasoning:** A garish or default palette (raw yellow/red)
signals "discount," undermining trust in a category where credibility
(licence, registration) is the core sell; a considered, curated palette
signals "vetted, professional agency."

**Creative reasoning:** One grading commitment per ad — mixing warm and
cool casts in the same frame (the V12 green-cast failure) reads as
unresolved, not intentional.

**Examples:** Saudi Arabia → Desert Gold. UAE → Blue + Gold. Kuwait → Deep
Blue. Qatar → Burgundy. Bahrain → Red + White.

**Permanent rule:** Choose one curated, destination-and-mood-matched
palette per ad and commit to it fully — never a raw default primary
palette, and never two unreconciled colour temperatures in the same
frame.

## 9. Information hierarchy

**Decision:** In what order, and at what relative visual weight, does the
reader encounter headline → hero → positions → salary/benefits →
interview → trust?

**Inputs considered:** The winning opportunity lever; content density
(sparse single-role vs. dense mass-hiring); channel (newspaper vs. social
feed, see §16).

**Trade-offs:** A hierarchy that gives every section roughly equal weight
is "fair" to all the content but produces no clear reading path — nothing
stands out. A hierarchy that over-weights the hook can leave supporting
facts (benefits, interview details) under-visible, undermining the close.

**Why one option wins:** A single, clear descending order — hook/hero
first, core offer (positions/salary/benefits) second, action (interview/
CTA) third, trust (agency/registration/QR) fourth — wins because it
mirrors the actual decision sequence a candidate goes through: notice →
evaluate → act → verify.

**Candidate psychology:** Readers build trust progressively; leading with
trust elements (registration numbers, agency history) before the offer
itself is backwards — nobody verifies an opportunity they haven't yet
found compelling.

**Commercial reasoning:** A hierarchy matched to the candidate's actual
decision sequence maximizes the odds the reader reaches the CTA with
enough motivation and information to act.

**Creative reasoning:** Hierarchy is expressed through size, weight,
colour contrast, and position together — not through any single device
alone.

**Examples:** Hook/hero zone at the top and largest; positions/salary in
the dominant mid-body; interview/CTA prominent but secondary; trust
footer present, sized appropriately, but never competing with the offer
above it.

**Permanent rule:** The visual order is always notice (hook/hero) →
evaluate (offer: positions/salary/benefits) → act (interview/CTA) →
verify (trust footer) — this sequence is never reordered or flattened.

## 10. White-space usage

**Decision:** How much of the canvas, if any, is left visually "empty"?

**Inputs considered:** The category's cost-per-space convention (paid
classified space); content density; whether empty space is serving a
deliberate compositional purpose (a breathing zone around the hero) vs.
simply unused.

**Trade-offs:** Generous white space can look premium and uncluttered in
other advertising categories, but in this category — priced and consumed
like paid classified space — unjustified empty space reads as wasted
opportunity rather than restraint. Zero white space risks feeling
cramped if not paired with strong hierarchy.

**Why one option wins:** Near-full canvas utilization wins, but only when
paired with strong hierarchy (§9) and hero protection (§3) — density
without hierarchy is clutter; hierarchy without density is waste.

**Candidate psychology:** In this category, dense-but-organized reads as
"serious, real opportunity, lots on offer"; empty space reads as "thin
offer" or "unfinished," not as premium restraint.

**Commercial reasoning:** Space is a paid resource; unjustified empty
canvas is a mandatory rejection criterion (see §21) because it represents
wasted spend on every rendering.

**Creative reasoning:** The exception is the hero's protected zone (§3) —
deliberate visual breathing room around the hero subject is not "dead
space," it's a compositional choice serving the hero, and must be
distinguished from unused margin/gutter waste.

**Examples:** A footer address sized to fill its full width rather than
sitting small in leftover space; a dense position table using every
column rather than leaving a blank quarter of the canvas.

**Permanent rule:** No layout may carry more than incidental unjustified
empty canvas — space is filled with content or with a deliberate,
hero-serving compositional gap, never left blank by default.

## 11. Density management

**Decision:** How does the layout adapt as the number of positions,
benefits, and interview events grows from one to dozens?

**Inputs considered:** Position count; benefit count; interview event
count; whether the requirement is a single-role box, a moderate multi-
role box, or a mass-hiring poster.

**Trade-offs:** A single fixed layout that's tuned for low density
clips or truncates as content grows (see failure library). A layout that's
tuned for high density from the start wastes space and impact on genuinely
sparse, single-role requirements.

**Why one option wins:** Density-adaptive layout selection wins — sparse
requirements get a bold, high-impact single-role treatment; dense
requirements get a grouped, multi-column table treatment; the layout
family is chosen by content volume, not fixed per campaign type.

**Candidate psychology:** A sparse requirement shown in a dense-table
layout looks under-filled and thin; a dense requirement shown in a
sparse layout looks cramped or truncated — both undermine the read.

**Commercial reasoning:** Correctly matching layout family to density
means every requirement, from one role to over a hundred, publishes at
full quality without manual rework per campaign.

**Creative reasoning:** Grouping (§13/dense lists) and font-size scaling
are density's two primary tools — never truncation.

**Examples:** A one-role, twenty-vacancy Bus Driver requirement → bold,
single-role box. A 129-position multi-division requirement → grouped,
multi-column masonry.

**Permanent rule:** Layout family and type scale are chosen by grounded
content volume — never a fixed template applied regardless of how much
content actually exists.

## 12. Trust placement

**Decision:** Where, and how prominently, do agency identity, licence/
registration, and the verification QR appear?

**Inputs considered:** Category trust requirements (licensed agency,
government registration); the offer's own strength (does it need more or
less reassurance); footer space available at the chosen density.

**Trade-offs:** Trust elements placed too early or too large crowd out
and precede the offer, inverting the candidate's actual decision sequence
(§9). Trust elements placed too small, too late, or compressed under
space pressure become illegible or clip entirely, defeating their
purpose.

**Why one option wins:** A single, protected footer/trust zone wins —
present in full on every ad, proportioned to a defined weight, never
expanding to crowd the offer and never compressing below a legibility
floor (especially the QR, which fails completely rather than gracefully
when compressed).

**Candidate psychology:** Trust is verified after interest is earned, not
before — a candidate checks the registration number and scans the QR once
they're already considering applying, not as their first action.

**Commercial reasoning:** Trust elements are mandatory (licence,
verification QR) and legally/commercially necessary, but their function
is confirmation, not persuasion — they must never consume the persuasive
real estate the offer needs.

**Creative reasoning:** One designated trust zone, never duplicated
elsewhere on the canvas without a distinct, deliberate reason.

**Examples:** Agency logo → name → MEA/RA registration → QR verification
→ since-year, in that order, confined to the footer, present but
proportionate.

**Permanent rule:** Trust elements occupy one protected, proportionate
footer zone — always present, never expanding to dominate, never
compressed below legibility, and never duplicated without cause.

## 13. CTA prominence

**Decision:** How visually loud is the call-to-action relative to the
offer content above it?

**Inputs considered:** CTA channel available (walk-in, email, WhatsApp,
phone, or mixed); whether a real, date-bound urgency signal exists;
overall ad density (a dense poster needs the CTA to still cut through).

**Trade-offs:** An overly loud, generic CTA banner ("URGENT HIRING")
without real urgency behind it looks cheap and undercuts a premium tone.
An overly quiet CTA gets lost in a dense layout and fails to convert
interest into contact.

**Why one option wins:** CTA prominence scales with the genuineness and
specificity of the urgency behind it — a real walk-in date with a
specific venue earns a high-priority, loud treatment; a standard
"apply by email" CTA earns a clear but calmer, medium-priority treatment.

**Candidate psychology:** A candidate who has read this far wants one
unambiguous next action; ambiguity or excessive volume both cost
conversions — the former from confusion, the latter from distrust.

**Commercial reasoning:** The CTA is the ad's actual conversion point;
its priority should be tuned to maximize genuine response, not to look
urgent for its own sake.

**Creative reasoning:** CTA visual treatment (colour, size, badge/banner
device) should match the emotional register (§5) already established —
an urgency-led ad earns an urgency-styled CTA; a prestige-led ad earns a
calmer, confident CTA treatment.

**Examples:** Royal Palace Hotel (real walk-in date) → high-priority,
walk-in-styled CTA. Bilfinger (standard email contact, no spot-selection
urgency) → medium-priority, clear but unforced CTA.

**Permanent rule:** CTA prominence is proportional to genuine, grounded
urgency — never maximized by default, and never so quiet it's missed in
a dense layout.

## 14. Benefits prioritization

**Decision:** Which grounded benefits are shown, and in what order?

**Inputs considered:** Every benefit actually present in the source data;
established category priority order (salary, overtime, food,
accommodation, transport, medical, insurance, air ticket, leave, contract
duration).

**Trade-offs:** Showing every benefit at equal weight creates a long,
undifferentiated list. Showing only a curated subset risks omitting a
benefit a specific candidate segment cares most about, and understates
the offer (the V12 "thin benefits" failure).

**Why one option wins:** Every grounded benefit is shown — none omitted —
but ranked by category priority so the highest-impact benefits (salary,
overtime, food) read first and smaller ones follow, rather than being
selectively curated down.

**Candidate psychology:** A visibly complete, well-ranked benefits list
reads as "a generous, fully-disclosed offer"; a sparse list — even with
the same underlying value — reads as thin regardless of the reason.

**Commercial reasoning:** Under-displaying grounded benefits directly
suppresses conversion versus benchmark ads that stack every advantage
they can genuinely claim.

**Creative reasoning:** Priority ranking, not omission, solves density —
the highest-priority benefits get the most visual weight; lower-priority
ones can be smaller or grouped, but never dropped.

**Examples:** Bilfinger's benefit set (Basic Salary, Overtime, Food,
Insurance) shown as a complete, ranked stack rather than two isolated
chips.

**Permanent rule:** Every grounded benefit is displayed, ranked by
category priority — never a subset chosen for space convenience.

## 15. Mobile-first reading path

**Decision:** What must remain fully legible when the canvas is viewed at
typical phone-feed scale, and what is allowed to shrink?

**Inputs considered:** Primary distribution channel (WhatsApp, Instagram,
Facebook feeds, viewed small); element criticality to the decision
sequence (§9); minimum legible type size at that scale.

**Trade-offs:** Treating every element as equally "must survive" makes
mobile scaling impossible without cramming. Treating everything as
flexible risks losing genuinely critical information (headline,
positions, salary) at small size.

**Why one option wins:** A two-tier system wins: a small, non-negotiable
"must survive" set (headline, positions, interview/action, salary) that
never shrinks below legibility, and a "may shrink" set (address,
registration line, QR caption) that can compress but still has an
enforced legibility floor.

**Candidate psychology:** The primary viewing context for this category is
a phone-sized feed, not a full-size print or desktop preview — an ad that
only works at full size fails in its actual, dominant use case.

**Commercial reasoning:** Losing legibility on the highest-value elements
(headline, salary) at the platform's default viewing size directly costs
conversions, regardless of how good the ad looks at full size.

**Creative reasoning:** Mobile-first design is not a separate pass — it's
a constraint applied during the original layout decision, not a
downscaling afterthought.

**Examples:** RA registration number and QR caption may shrink but must
remain legible; headline, position titles, and salary numerals never
shrink past a fixed floor.

**Permanent rule:** Every element is classified must-survive or
may-shrink at design time, with an enforced legibility floor for both —
mobile legibility is never an afterthought check.

## 16. Newspaper vs. social-media strategy

**Decision:** Does the ad follow the DTP-newspaper grammar or the social-
square/portrait visual-hero grammar — and what changes between them?

**Inputs considered:** Target channel (print classified vs. WhatsApp/
Instagram/LinkedIn feed); content density (newspaper grammar handles very
high density better); expected viewing context (print/PDF at leisure vs.
a fast scroll).

**Trade-offs:** DTP-newspaper grammar (white newsprint, bordered grid,
dense tabular positions) is unmatched for high-density, many-position
requirements and for the category's established credibility signal, but
its fine detail and small type don't survive a fast social scroll as
well. Visual-hero/social grammar (large hero image, bold headline,
minimal density) is built for the one-second scroll-stop but can't carry
dozens of positions without becoming a wall of text.

**Why one option wins:** The channel and the content shape jointly decide
— high-density, many-position requirements lean DTP-newspaper grammar
regardless of distribution channel (because it's the only grammar built to
carry that density well); sparse-to-moderate, emotionally-led
requirements lean visual-hero grammar for social distribution.

**Candidate psychology:** A newspaper-classified reader is already
committed to reading (they're scanning the page deliberately); a social-
feed viewer is mid-scroll and must be stopped first — the two contexts
demand different opening moves even when the underlying offer is
identical.

**Commercial reasoning:** Using the wrong grammar for the channel wastes
either the newspaper grammar's density advantage or the social grammar's
scroll-stop advantage.

**Creative reasoning:** Both grammars share the same underlying decision
system (hero, hook, hierarchy, trust) — they differ in visual language and
density tolerance, not in the decisions being made.

**Examples:** A 30-position Halliburton requirement suits DTP-newspaper's
tabular density even for social distribution. A single-role, emotionally-
led Bus Driver ad suits the visual-hero grammar's scroll-stop strength.

**Permanent rule:** Grammar (DTP-newspaper vs. visual-hero/social) is
chosen by content density and channel jointly — density above roughly a
dozen roles favors newspaper grammar regardless of channel; sparse,
emotionally-led content favors visual-hero grammar for social channels.

## 17. Industry-specific visual language

**Decision:** What environment, mood, and colour temperature does each
industry imply by default?

**Inputs considered:** Industry classification (oil & gas, construction,
shipyard, hospitality, healthcare, aviation, manufacturing, renewable
energy); the industry's own attractiveness/prestige level; typical work
environment (indoor/outdoor, scale, atmosphere).

**Trade-offs:** A rich, industry-specific visual language (refinery
towers for oil & gas, luxury interiors for hospitality) is far more
credible than a generic "professional workplace" default, but requires
confident, correct industry classification — a wrong match is worse than
a generic one (see the failure library's scene-collision entries).

**Why one option wins:** Specific industry visual language wins whenever
classification confidence is high; the generic fallback exists
specifically for the cases where no confident specific match applies —
it is a safety net, not a default preference.

**Candidate psychology:** Candidates recognize their own trade instantly
in an accurate environment; it's one of the fastest "this is for me"
signals available.

**Commercial reasoning:** Industry-accurate imagery differentiates the ad
from generic competitor templates and reinforces that this specific
opportunity was represented seriously.

**Creative reasoning:** Each industry pairs a default environment, hero
type, and colour mood — oil & gas skews warm/golden/scale-driven,
hospitality skews clean/refined/premium, construction skews structural/
dynamic.

**Examples:** Oil & gas → refinery, golden hour, desert gold. Hospitality
→ refined interior, soft diffused light, clean corporate grading.
Construction → cranes and rising structures, structural drama.

**Permanent rule:** Every industry carries a default environment/mood/
colour pairing, applied whenever classification is confident; a generic
fallback is used only when it isn't — never as a first choice.

## 18. Country-specific psychology

**Decision:** What emotional tone and premium colour association does
each destination country carry?

**Inputs considered:** The destination's relative prestige tier among
Gulf destinations; its currency; cultural/market perception among the
candidate audience (career-building vs. income-focused vs. stability-
focused).

**Trade-offs:** Treating all Gulf destinations identically misses real,
learned differences in how candidates perceive them (Saudi Arabia's
opportunity narrative differs from Bahrain's stability narrative). Over-
differentiating risks inconsistency across campaigns for the same
country.

**Why one option wins:** A locked per-country table wins — each
destination has one fixed premium colour and one fixed emotional tone,
applied consistently across every campaign to that country, rather than
re-decided per ad.

**Candidate psychology:** Candidates already hold associations with each
Gulf destination from prior exposure (peers who've worked there, general
reputation); the ad should align with, not fight, that existing
association.

**Commercial reasoning:** Consistent per-country treatment builds
recognizable, trustworthy visual language over time — a Saudi Arabia ad
should always feel like a Saudi Arabia ad, regardless of which agency or
employer is featured.

**Creative reasoning:** Country tone (§18) and industry tone (§17)
combine — country sets the emotional register and base palette, industry
sets the specific environment within it.

**Examples:** Saudi Arabia → Desert Gold / Opportunity. UAE → Blue + Gold
/ Modern Career. Kuwait → Deep Blue / High Income. Qatar → Burgundy /
Premium. Bahrain → Red + White / Stable.

**Permanent rule:** Country-to-tone-and-colour mapping is a fixed,
locked table, applied consistently — never re-improvised per campaign.

## 19. Premium vs. mass-hiring advertisements

**Decision:** Does this requirement read as an executive/premium
opportunity or a mass-hiring/walk-in drive — and how does that change the
whole design language?

**Inputs considered:** Headcount (single/few roles vs. dozens); role
seniority signal; urgency (spot selection, walk-in dates) vs. considered
application process; industry prestige tier.

**Trade-offs:** A premium treatment (restrained colour, generous
composition, fewer but larger elements) undersells a genuine mass-hiring
drive's urgency and energy. A mass-hiring treatment (dense, urgent,
high-energy) undersells a premium single-role opportunity's exclusivity.

**Why one option wins:** The "personality" of the ad (executive, corporate,
premium, mass hiring, walk-in drive, shutdown, mega project, urgent
mobilization, luxury hospitality, government, healthcare) is selected from
the actual grounded signals — headcount, urgency, and industry together —
never assumed from a template.

**Candidate psychology:** A premium role attracts candidates evaluating
fit and prestige, who respond to restraint and specificity; a mass-hiring
drive attracts candidates evaluating speed and certainty, who respond to
urgency and clarity of process.

**Commercial reasoning:** Mismatching personality to reality
under-communicates urgency for genuinely urgent mass drives, or cheapens
genuinely premium single-role opportunities.

**Creative reasoning:** Personality governs density tolerance, colour
saturation, and CTA styling together — it's a single coherent register,
not a set of independent choices.

**Examples:** A 20-role Bus Driver walk-in requirement → urgent
mobilization / mass hiring personality, dense and energetic. A 2-role
Bilfinger shutdown requirement with a real salary → shutdown / premium
personality, more restrained and specific.

**Permanent rule:** Ad personality (premium vs. mass-hiring, and the
finer-grained personalities within each) is derived from headcount +
urgency + industry signals together — never assumed from the archetype
alone.

## 20. Benchmark comparison methodology

**Decision:** What is an ad actually being judged against?

**Inputs considered:** Real published classifieds (grammar reference
only); KAI's own locked rule set (the actual acceptance bar); the
specific campaign's own grounded facts (nothing can be judged against
facts it doesn't have).

**Trade-offs:** Judging against a single real reference agency's box risks
baking that agency's specific habits into every campaign, including ones
for entirely different tenants. Judging with no reference at all risks
losing touch with what a genuinely credible classified looks like.

**Why one option wins:** Real references inform *grammar* only (what a
credible DTP classified generally looks like); the actual pass/fail
benchmark is always KAI's own rule set, applied to this campaign's own
grounded data — never a specific competitor agency's box, and never a
different campaign's data.

**Candidate psychology:** Not directly relevant to this decision — this
is a production-discipline decision, not a candidate-facing one.

**Commercial reasoning:** A benchmark tied to KAI's own generic rule set
scales correctly across every tenant and client; a benchmark tied to one
reference agency does not.

**Creative reasoning:** Grammar research keeps the system grounded in real
category conventions without becoming derivative of any one publisher.

**Examples:** Studying Assignments Abroad Times boxes for grammar (bordered
grids, reversed bars, dotted leaders) while never importing any specific
agency's name, phone number, or layout quirk into the generic system.

**Permanent rule:** Real classifieds are grammar research; KAI's own rule
set, applied to this campaign's own data, is the only pass/fail benchmark.

## 21. Self-review checklist

Before any advertisement is considered complete, review against every item
below. A failure on any item routes back to the relevant decision above.

1. Is there exactly one dominant hero claim, or are two-plus claims
   competing for lead position? (§1, §4)
2. Does the visual hero match the chosen emotional register and industry?
   (§3, §5, §6)
3. Is the background scene verifiably correct for the grounded industry —
   no substring-collision mismatches? (§6, §17)
4. Does every text element map to one of the five fixed typographic
   roles? (§7)
5. Is the colour palette a single, curated, destination-matched
   commitment with no competing cast? (§8)
6. Does the reading order follow notice → evaluate → act → verify, with no
   section out of sequence? (§9)
7. Is there any unjustified empty canvas, or any element under-filling
   its allotted space? (§10)
8. Is any content truncated, clipped, or abbreviated rather than resized?
   (§11, and the failure library's truncation/clipping entries)
9. Are all grounded benefits present and ranked, none omitted for space?
   (§14)
10. Is the CTA's visual prominence proportional to a real, grounded
    urgency signal? (§13)
11. Is the trust footer present, proportionate, and unduplicated — is the
    QR fully visible and uncompressed? (§12, and the QR-clipping /
    agency-duplication failure entries)
12. Does every must-survive element remain legible at mobile-feed scale?
    (§15)
13. Is the ad's personality (premium vs. mass-hiring, and its finer
    variant) consistent with the actual headcount/urgency/industry
    signals? (§19)
14. Is every fact on the canvas traceable to a grounded source — nothing
    invented, nothing guessed (currency, benefits, dates, logos)?
15. Would this ad, judged against KAI's own rule set (not a competitor
    reference), earn a genuine "yes" from a paying client? (§20)

## 22. Publish / reject criteria

**Decision:** Does this advertisement ship as-is, go to creative review, or
get rejected and redesigned?

**Inputs considered:** A composite commercial quality score across scroll-
stop, commercial appeal, candidate psychology, information hierarchy,
colour harmony, typography, trust, mobile readability, brand quality, and
publish readiness; the presence of any catastrophic defect (fabricated/
gibberish text in generated imagery, clipped content, overlapping text,
wrong industry scene, unjustified trust duplication, missing verification
identity, severe dead-canvas).

**Trade-offs:** A high composite score with one catastrophic defect present
must still fail — catastrophic defects are absolute gates, not
score-averaged. A merely mediocre score with no catastrophic defect is a
"send back for revision" case, not an outright rejection — it's fixable
within the same framework.

**Why one option wins:** Catastrophic defects are checked first and are
binary: any single one present is an automatic reject regardless of
overall score. Only once the ad is clear of catastrophic defects does the
composite score decide between auto-approve, creative review, and reject.

**Candidate psychology:** Not directly relevant — this is the production
gate a candidate never sees, but it exists to protect the trust and
quality every other decision in this playbook was built to earn.

**Commercial reasoning:** A single catastrophic defect (a wrong industry
scene, a clipped QR, fabricated text in the background) can undo the
credibility every other correct decision built — it must be an absolute
gate, not something a strong score can outweigh.

**Creative reasoning:** The scoring dimensions map directly to the
decisions in this playbook (§1–§19) — a low score is diagnostic, pointing
back to which decision needs revisiting, not just a pass/fail number.

**Examples:** A technically well-scored ad with a clipped QR still fails —
the QR gate is absolute. A merely 7.5-average ad with thin benefits and no
catastrophic defect goes to creative review for a benefits/hook revision,
not an outright rejection.

**Permanent rule:** Catastrophic defects are checked first and are
absolute rejection gates regardless of score. With no catastrophic defect
present: **95–100 → auto-approve · 90–94 → creative review · below 90 →
reject and redesign.**

## 23. The deterministic Creative Director Decision Flow

Every advertisement must be evaluated through this sequence, in this
order, before rendering. Each stage's output is a locked input to the
next — nothing downstream may contradict a decision already locked
upstream.

```
STAGE 0 — Truth Gate
  → Confirm every fact to be used is grounded in the source.
  → Any fact needed but not present is omitted, never invented.
  → FAIL here stops everything else.

STAGE 1 — Opportunity Ranking (§1, §2)
  → Rank Country / Salary / Industry / Project / Employer / Positions /
    Benefits / Interview / Trust by grounded strength for this campaign.
  → Lock exactly ONE hero lever.

STAGE 2 — Emotional Direction (§5)
  → Derive the single dominant emotional register from the locked hero
    lever (money / opportunity / prestige / urgency / stability).

STAGE 3 — Candidate Hook (§4)
  → Compose the one specific, grounded headline claim expressing the
    hero lever + emotional register together.
  → Reject any hook that is generic or unsupported by a grounded fact.

STAGE 4 — Visual Story + Hero Subject (§3, §6, §17)
  → Select the industry-matched environment (specific match preferred,
    generic fallback only if no confident match exists).
  → Select the hero subject type matching the emotional register.
  → Verify the environment cannot be confused with an unrelated industry
    (explicit substring/keyword collision check).

STAGE 5 — Country + Colour Commitment (§8, §18)
  → Apply the locked country → emotional tone → premium colour mapping.
  → Commit to one colour grading; explicitly ban any competing cast.

STAGE 6 — Personality + Density Classification (§11, §19)
  → Classify ad personality from headcount + urgency + industry.
  → Classify content density (sparse / medium / high) from actual
    position/benefit/interview counts.
  → Select layout family (single-role box / structured / dense
    table-grid / grouped multi-vacancy poster) from density.

STAGE 7 — Channel + Grammar Selection (§16)
  → Select DTP-newspaper grammar or visual-hero/social grammar from
    density + target channel jointly.

STAGE 8 — Information Hierarchy Assembly (§7, §9, §10, §14)
  → Order sections: notice (hook/hero) → evaluate (positions/salary/
    benefits, all grounded benefits ranked, none omitted) → act
    (interview/CTA) → verify (trust footer).
  → Assign every text element one of the five fixed typographic roles.
  → Fill available canvas; eliminate unjustified empty space.

STAGE 9 — Trust + CTA Placement (§12, §13)
  → Place agency identity, registration, and verification QR in one
    protected, proportionate footer zone.
  → Set CTA prominence proportional to genuine grounded urgency.

STAGE 10 — Mobile Legibility Pass (§15)
  → Classify every element must-survive or may-shrink.
  → Verify legibility floors are met for both classes at feed scale.

STAGE 11 — Self-Review Checklist (§21)
  → Run all fifteen checklist items.
  → Any failure routes back to the specific stage above that owns it.

STAGE 12 — Catastrophic Defect Gate
  → Check for: fabricated/gibberish imagery text, clipped content,
    overlapping elements, wrong/contradictory industry scene, missing
    verification identity, unjustified trust duplication, severe
    unexplained dead canvas.
  → ANY match here is an automatic reject — stop, do not score.

STAGE 13 — Composite Commercial Score (§22)
  → Score across scroll-stop, commercial appeal, candidate psychology,
    information hierarchy, colour harmony, typography, trust, mobile
    readability, brand quality, publish readiness.
  → 95–100 → AUTO-APPROVE
  → 90–94  → CREATIVE REVIEW (targeted revision against the specific
              low-scoring dimension, re-enter at the owning stage)
  → <90    → REJECT AND REDESIGN (re-enter at Stage 1)
```

This flow is the permanent operating sequence of the Creative Director
Brain: every advertisement, for every tenant, in every channel and
archetype, passes through all thirteen stages, in this order, before it
is ever rendered.
