# Creative Director Failure Library

**Status:** Permanent — feeds the KAI Creative Director Brain
**Nature:** Every commercial and creative failure encountered while
designing KAI Ads advertisements (Halliburton, Bilfinger, Saudi Aramco,
Bus Driver, Royal Palace Hotel; Visual Hero, Structured Professional, High
Density, DTP Newspaper archetypes; B/W and Colour DTP variants), recorded
as a permanent rejection catalogue. No prompts, no code, no implementation,
no architecture — lessons only.

Each entry: Failure ID · Campaign · what failed · why it failed
commercially · root cause · how it was discovered · how it was corrected ·
the permanent rule · category.

---

## FL-001 — Document instead of advertisement

**Campaign:** Early flat-card pass (multiple campaigns).

**What failed:** A layout of plain white cards, thin borders, no
compositional drama — every fact present, nothing designed to sell.

**Why it failed commercially:** Scored 4/10 and was described as
"a textbook, not an advertisement." Recruitment classifieds compete for
attention against other classifieds; a flat, undecorated layout has zero
scroll-stopping power.

**Root cause:** Density (fitting every fact) was treated as sufficient on
its own, with no separate design pass for visual weight.

**How it was discovered:** Direct client scoring (4/10) with the explicit
verdict quoted above.

**How it was corrected:** Rebuilt with gradient headers, drop shadows, a
warm background tone, and deliberate section segmentation, while staying
inside the white-newsprint rule.

**Permanent rule:** Full information density is necessary but not
sufficient. Every ad needs a separate, explicit pass for visual weight
(colour, contrast, depth) or it reads as a form, not a sale.

**Category:** Composition.

## FL-002 — Employer dominating opportunity

**Campaign:** Bilfinger.

**What failed:** The ad opened with the contractor's name ("BILFINGER") as
the dominant headline element.

**Why it failed commercially:** Bilfinger is a credible but not a
candidate-recognizable brand; leading with it wastes the highest-value
canvas real estate on a fact that does not motivate a reader to keep
scrolling.

**Root cause:** The layout defaulted to "employer field = headline" instead
of ranking which grounded fact actually had the most pull.

**How it was discovered:** Named directly as weakness #3 in the V12
creative-director verdict: "no dominant hook — it opens with a brand, not
a reason."

**How it was corrected:** Hero position is now earned only by whichever
lever (country prestige, salary, or a genuinely recognizable "magnet"
employer) has the strongest grounded pull; a credible-but-unrecognized
employer is demoted to a trust/credibility line.

**Permanent rule:** The employer name earns the headline only when it is
an established candidate-magnet brand. Otherwise country, salary, or
project scale leads, and the employer becomes a trust signal.

**Category:** Psychology / Hierarchy.

## FL-003 — Dead canvas

**Campaign:** Multiple early passes (structured/typography archetypes).

**What failed:** Layouts left blank margins, gutters, and under-filled
footer zones.

**Why it failed commercially:** In a category priced by column-space,
unused canvas is wasted spend, and empty zones read as an unfinished or
cheap production.

**Root cause:** Elements were sized to a comfortable default rather than
to the space actually available to them.

**How it was discovered:** Explicit client correction: "every inch costs …
no blank space," plus the general constitution rule that >~20% unjustified
dead canvas is a mandatory rejection.

**How it was corrected:** Font sizes and block dimensions were increased
to reach full-bleed density; the footer address rule was tightened so
address text always spans the entire footer width rather than floating
small in leftover space.

**Permanent rule:** Canvas space is a paid resource. No layout may leave
meaningful blank area; elements are sized up to fill their allotted
space, never left at a "safe" smaller default.

**Category:** Composition.

## FL-004 — Weak CTA

**Campaign:** V12 Bilfinger and earlier passes.

**What failed:** The call-to-action relied on a generic device — an
"URGENT HIRING" banner — rather than a specific, actionable instruction
tied to a real interview date, venue, or contact channel.

**Why it failed commercially:** A generic urgency banner reads as
decoration, not instruction; candidates scan for *what to do next*
(walk-in date, email, WhatsApp), and a vague banner doesn't answer that
and undercuts the premium tone the rest of the ad was going for.

**Root cause:** Urgency was treated as a standalone visual device instead
of being tied to the actual grounded CTA channel (walk-in / email /
WhatsApp / phone) and its priority.

**How it was discovered:** Named directly in the V12 verdict: "'URGENT
HIRING' reads slightly cheap and undercuts the premium tone."

**How it was corrected:** CTA is now derived from the grounded interview/
contact data and assigned an explicit kind (walk-in, email, WhatsApp,
phone, or mixed) and priority — urgency banners are only used when a real,
date-bound urgency signal exists.

**Permanent rule:** A CTA banner must be grounded in a real channel and
real urgency signal — never a decorative "URGENT" device with nothing
specific behind it.

**Category:** CTA.

## FL-005 — Tiny QR / RA number vanish at mobile size

**Campaign:** Poster/structured layouts viewed at mobile scale.

**What failed:** The RA/registration number and the QR scan caption became
unreadable, and benefit chips became visually tight, once the canvas was
scaled down to a typical phone-feed size.

**Why it failed commercially:** Recruitment ads are consumed primarily on
WhatsApp/Instagram/Facebook feeds at small display sizes; an illegible
trust element (RA number, QR caption) at that size defeats the entire
purpose of including it.

**Root cause:** Trust and fine-print elements were sized for a full-canvas
desktop view without a defined minimum legible size at mobile scale.

**How it was discovered:** Explicit mobile-readability review, scored 8.0
in the V12 audit with the specific finding that RA number/QR caption
vanish when small.

**How it was corrected:** A mobile-survival list was introduced —
headline, positions, interview/action, and salary must always survive
shrinkage; address, registration line, and QR caption are the elements
allowed to shrink, but never below a legible floor.

**Permanent rule:** Every element carries an explicit mobile-survival or
mobile-shrink classification; trust elements that may shrink must still
have an enforced legibility floor, never an unbounded one.

**Category:** Mobile Readability.

## FL-006 — QR clipping

**Campaign:** Poster/structured layouts under footer space pressure.

**What failed:** When footer space was compressed by other content, the
verification QR risked being cropped at the canvas edge or overlapped by
adjacent footer elements rather than remaining fully visible and scannable.

**Why it failed commercially:** A partially clipped QR cannot be scanned
at all — it doesn't degrade gracefully like text does, it simply fails,
breaking the one trust mechanism that lets a candidate independently
verify the ad.

**Root cause:** The QR was treated as just another footer element subject
to the same flexible sizing as everything else, instead of being given a
protected, non-negotiable slot.

**How it was discovered:** Identified during footer/trust composition
review as a structural risk category (the same class of failure as the
hero-collapse issue in FL-011, applied to the footer instead of the
hero).

**How it was corrected:** The QR and its caption were given a fixed
minimum footprint inside the footer that other footer content must flow
around, never compress.

**Permanent rule:** The verification QR must always have a protected,
uncompromised minimum size and full visibility — it is the one element
that fails completely rather than gracefully when compressed.

**Category:** Trust / Mobile Readability.

## FL-007 — Agency trust elements duplicated across the canvas

**Campaign:** Poster/structured layouts with multiple trust touchpoints.

**What failed:** Agency identity elements (logo, name, registration)
appeared more than once across the canvas without a clear, deliberate
reason.

**Why it failed commercially:** Repeated trust elements read as
compositional padding — filling space rather than adding information —
and dilute the footer's authority as *the* single place a candidate looks
to verify legitimacy.

**Root cause:** Trust elements were being placed wherever space was
available rather than being confined to one designated trust zone.

**How it was discovered:** Flagged explicitly as a catastrophic-defect
category in the visual QA check: "agency trust elements unjustifiably
repeated across the canvas."

**How it was corrected:** Agency identity is confined to one designated
footer/trust zone per ad; any secondary appearance (e.g. a small header
logo) must be a distinct, deliberate role (brand mark vs. verification
footer), never a redundant copy.

**Permanent rule:** Agency trust elements appear in exactly one designated
zone unless a second appearance serves a clearly distinct role. Unjustified
duplication is a hard defect, not a stylistic choice.

**Category:** Trust / Branding.

## FL-008 — Wrong hierarchy: footer address undersized in its own space

**Campaign:** Early DTP passes.

**What failed:** The agency address was set at a small, fixed size that
left the surrounding footer width mostly blank.

**Why it failed commercially:** Combines a dead-canvas failure (FL-003)
with a legibility failure — the one contact detail a candidate needs after
deciding to apply was rendered smaller than the space around it justified.

**Root cause:** Address text used a fixed font size rather than a size
computed to fill its footer width.

**How it was discovered:** Direct client correction requiring the address
to "fill the entire footer space," with font size scaled accordingly.

**How it was corrected:** The footer-fill rule was locked: address text is
sized up until it spans the full footer width, never left at a small
fixed size regardless of available space.

**Permanent rule:** Footer/contact text is sized to its available width,
not to a fixed default — under-filling a wide footer is as much a
hierarchy failure as overcrowding a narrow one.

**Category:** Hierarchy / Typography.

## FL-009 — Poor colour harmony (unreconciled colour story)

**Campaign:** V12 Bilfinger.

**What failed:** A cool green/olive sky treatment and a warm gold
treatment were both present in the same canvas without being reconciled
into one committed story.

**Why it failed commercially:** Two competing colour temperatures in one
frame read as visually unresolved — it muddies the premium identity the
gold treatment was trying to build.

**Root cause:** Background generation and colour-mood selection were not
locked to a single, destination-specific grading commitment.

**How it was discovered:** Direct finding in the V12 verdict: colour
balance scored 7.5, "cool green sky isn't reconciled with the gold — not
one unified colour story."

**How it was corrected:** Colour mood is now locked per destination/
industry before background generation (e.g. Saudi Arabia → Desert Gold),
and the generation brief explicitly bans a competing cool cast.

**Permanent rule:** One colour grading commitment per ad, chosen up front
and enforced through to the finished canvas — never two unreconciled
colour temperatures in the same frame.

**Category:** Colour.

## FL-010 — Green cast specifically

**Campaign:** V12 Bilfinger.

**What failed:** The generated background sky carried a cool green/olive
tint that fought the intended warm desert-gold treatment.

**Why it failed commercially:** Green is not part of any locked premium
palette for this category (desert gold, deep blue, burgundy, blue+gold);
an unintended green cast reads as an uncorrected AI-generation artifact
rather than a deliberate design choice.

**Root cause:** No explicit negative constraint against cool/green casts
existed in the background generation direction.

**How it was discovered:** Direct visual inspection during the V12 review,
called out as the specific mechanism behind FL-009's colour-harmony score.

**How it was corrected:** Colour-grading instructions now explicitly
prohibit any cool green or blue cast when a warm grading (e.g. desert
gold) has been committed to.

**Permanent rule:** Once a warm colour grading is locked for an ad, cool
casts (green/blue) are an explicit, named prohibition in the generation
direction — not just an implicit expectation.

**Category:** Colour.

## FL-011 — Structural collapse: hero shrinks under sibling pressure

**Campaign:** Multi-vacancy poster layout.

**What failed:** The hero image block collapsed to a sliver (~100px)
because adjacent blocks (position list, benefits) absorbed the available
vertical space as content grew.

**Why it failed commercially:** The hero is the single most important
scroll-stopping element on the canvas; shrinking it to near-invisibility
under content pressure defeats the entire purpose of a visual-led
composition.

**Root cause:** No block on the canvas had a protected minimum size —
every block could be compressed indefinitely by its neighbours.

**How it was discovered:** Direct visual inspection of a rendered poster
with a large position count.

**How it was corrected:** Structural zones (brand strip, hero, position
banner, apply strip, footer) were each given a fixed minimum share of the
canvas that dense content cannot cannibalize.

**Permanent rule:** The hero/visual-story zone carries a non-negotiable
minimum size. Dense content must flow within its own zone, never borrow
space from the hero's protected minimum.

**Category:** Composition.

## FL-012 — Content clipping under dense position lists

**Campaign:** Multi-vacancy poster (WTS Supervisor I/II/III and similar).

**What failed:** Long or numerous position rows were clipped at the
bottom or side edge of their container.

**Why it failed commercially:** A clipped position title is a lost fact —
worse than a small font, because the information disappears entirely
rather than just becoming harder to read.

**Root cause:** Row font size and container height were tuned to a
"typical" vacancy count rather than to the actual (larger) count present.

**How it was discovered:** Direct visual inspection of a rendered poster
with position counts above the design's original assumption.

**How it was corrected:** List font size and hero/body proportions were
reduced together (rather than clipping the list) so every row fits inside
its container in full.

**Permanent rule:** When a position list exceeds its container's assumed
capacity, the fix is smaller type or a taller container — never clipping,
truncation, or dropped rows.

**Category:** Hierarchy / Truth.

## FL-013 — AI-generated gibberish / pseudo-text

**Campaign:** Visual Hero background generation, all campaigns using
AI-generated imagery.

**What failed:** Risk of the AI-generated background canvas containing
corrupted words, misspellings, fake numbers, garbled letters, decorative
pseudo-text, or signage with readable text.

**Why it failed commercially:** Any readable text baked into the
generated background — even nonsense text — reads as a production defect
to the viewer and can visually collide with or contradict the real,
deterministic overlay text placed on top of it.

**Root cause:** Image generation models default to rendering plausible-
looking but meaningless text/signage unless explicitly instructed not to.

**How it was discovered:** Identified as a named, catastrophic-defect
category in the visual QA specification before it could ship in any
campaign.

**How it was corrected:** Every background generation brief carries an
explicit, comprehensive negative instruction against any readable text,
letters, numbers, logos, or pseudo-typographic elements — the canvas must
be purely visual; all real text comes from the deterministic overlay.

**Permanent rule:** AI-generated background imagery must never contain any
readable text of any kind, real or gibberish — this is a hard,
non-negotiable generation constraint, not a quality-scoring nuance.

**Category:** Truth / Composition.

## FL-014 — Wrong industry scene generally

**Campaign:** Cross-industry scene selection (general risk class).

**What failed:** Risk of an ad's background scene depicting an industry
environment that does not match the grounded industry field (e.g. an oil
& gas refinery shown for a healthcare or hospitality opportunity).

**Why it failed commercially:** A visibly wrong industry scene
contradicts the ad's own factual content before a candidate reads a
single word — it is a Truth Brain violation expressed visually rather
than in text.

**Root cause:** Industry-to-scene classification depended on keyword
matching against free-text industry descriptions, which is vulnerable to
mismatches (see FL-015, FL-016 for the two concrete instances found).

**How it was discovered:** Direct benchmarking of multiple campaigns
against their grounded industry field, which surfaced the two specific
collisions below.

**How it was corrected:** Industry classification rules were tightened so
scene selection always traces back to, and must not contradict, the
grounded industry field.

**Permanent rule:** The background scene must always visually match the
grounded industry. Any keyword-based scene classifier must be validated
against real industry names before being trusted, not assumed correct.

**Category:** Visual Story / Truth.

## FL-015 — Hospitality → Hospital collision

**Campaign:** Royal Palace Hotel.

**What failed:** A Hospitality-industry campaign (a hotel employer) was
classified into a hospital/healthcare visual scene instead of a hotel
scene.

**Why it failed commercially:** A hotel job candidate shown a hospital
scene sees an ad that visually contradicts its own employer and industry
— an immediate, visible trust break.

**Root cause:** The word "hospital" is a literal substring of
"Hospitality," and the healthcare-matching rule was checked before the
hotel-matching rule, so any Hospitality-industry input matched the
healthcare rule first.

**How it was discovered:** Direct benchmarking of the Royal Palace Hotel
campaign against its own grounded industry field, which produced a
hospital scene on first run.

**How it was corrected:** The hotel/hospitality classification rule was
reordered to run before the healthcare rule, so the longer, more specific
match wins.

**Permanent rule:** When classification keys can be substrings of each
other, check the longer/more specific term first — never let a shorter,
coincidentally-embedded word win the match.

**Category:** Visual Story / Truth.

## FL-016 — Refined → Refinery collision

**Campaign:** Royal Palace Hotel.

**What failed:** A hospitality scene description containing the ordinary
English word "refined" (as in "a refined, premium hospitality interior")
was classified into an oil & gas refinery environment.

**Why it failed commercially:** The resulting background brief described
"a vast oil & gas refinery" for a hotel recruitment ad — a direct,
visible contradiction of the actual industry, and a compounding instance
of the same trust break as FL-015 in a different location of the
pipeline.

**Root cause:** The oil & gas classification rule matched on the raw
substring "refin," which is also the root of the unrelated English word
"refined" — a keyword-substring collision distinct from, but the same
class of bug as, FL-015.

**How it was discovered:** Direct benchmarking of the Royal Palace Hotel
campaign, which surfaced the wrong environment description immediately
after the FL-015 fix, on the same campaign.

**How it was corrected:** The oil & gas match was made to explicitly
exclude the "…ed" ending, so "refinery/refining/refineries" still match
but "refined" does not.

**Permanent rule:** Any short root-word match ("refin," "hospital," etc.)
used for classification must be checked against genuinely unrelated
real words that happen to contain it as a substring, and excluded
explicitly where a collision exists.

**Category:** Visual Story / Truth.

## FL-017 — Weak benefits

**Campaign:** V12 Bilfinger.

**What failed:** Only "Basic Salary" was presented as a benefit, rendered
as two sparse chips.

**Why it failed commercially:** "Basic Salary" is not a benefit, it is the
baseline expectation; against benchmark ads stacking food, accommodation,
transport, medical, and overtime, a two-chip offer looks sparse and kills
desire at the close of the read.

**Root cause:** Benefits were displayed in whatever order/count the
source data happened to list, without a ranking or completeness check
against what was actually available in the source.

**How it was discovered:** Direct finding in the V12 verdict: "benefits
are thin and undersell the offer… kills desire at the close."

**How it was corrected:** Benefits are now ranked by priority (salary,
overtime, food, accommodation, transport, medical, insurance, air ticket,
leave, contract duration) and every grounded benefit present in the
source is surfaced, never a subset chosen arbitrarily.

**Permanent rule:** Every grounded benefit must be surfaced and ranked by
priority — never display fewer benefits than are actually present in the
source data.

**Category:** Psychology.

## FL-018 — Generic hooks

**Campaign:** V12 Bilfinger and earlier passes.

**What failed:** The ad's opening claim was a generic device (a brand
name, or a generic "URGENT HIRING" banner) rather than a specific,
grounded reason to apply.

**Why it failed commercially:** A generic hook informs without seducing —
it fails to answer the one question a scrolling candidate asks in the
first second: "what's in it for me, specifically."

**Root cause:** The headline defaulted to whichever field happened to be
first in the source data (usually employer name) rather than being chosen
by ranking the actual persuasive levers available.

**How it was discovered:** Direct finding in the V12 verdict, weakness
#3: "no dominant hook… it informs; it doesn't seduce."

**How it was corrected:** A dominant-hook selection step now explicitly
ranks available levers (salary → destination/sector prestige → magnet
employer) and commits to the strongest one as the headline claim.

**Permanent rule:** The headline hook must be the single strongest
grounded persuasive lever, explicitly selected — never a default field or
a generic urgency device standing in for a real reason to apply.

**Category:** Psychology.

## FL-019 — Excessive trust area crowding the offer

**Campaign:** Structured/footer-heavy layouts.

**What failed:** Risk of the trust footer (agency logo, name, full
address, registration line, QR, caption) expanding to consume space that
should belong to the actual job offer (positions, salary, benefits).

**Why it failed commercially:** Trust elements are necessary but not the
reason a candidate applies; letting them dominate the canvas at the
expense of the offer inverts the ad's actual persuasive priority.

**Root cause:** The footer had no defined proportional ceiling relative to
the rest of the canvas, so it could grow to accommodate every trust
element at full size regardless of offer content.

**How it was discovered:** Identified during footer/trust composition
design as a structural risk requiring an explicit, controlled footer
variant rather than an unconstrained one.

**How it was corrected:** The footer was defined as a flexible but
*controlled* variant system — trust elements (agency identity,
registration, QR) always present, but proportioned to a defined footer
weight rather than allowed to expand freely.

**Permanent rule:** The trust footer earns a proportionate, controlled
share of the canvas — never an unconstrained one that can crowd out the
actual offer content above it.

**Category:** Trust / Hierarchy.

## FL-020 — Poor mobile readability generally

**Campaign:** Cross-campaign (all archetypes viewed at feed scale).

**What failed:** Multiple elements (benefit chips, RA number, QR caption,
address) became difficult to read once the canvas was viewed at typical
mobile-feed scale, beyond the specific instance in FL-005.

**Why it failed commercially:** The primary consumption context for these
ads is a phone-sized feed (WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook), not a full-size
desktop preview — an ad that only reads well at full size fails in its
actual, primary viewing context.

**Root cause:** Design and review were happening at full canvas size
without a systematic check against a realistic mobile viewport.

**How it was discovered:** Explicit mobile-readability scoring dimension
in the V12 audit (scored 8.0, short of full marks) plus the specific
chip/RA/QR findings in FL-005.

**How it was corrected:** A mobile-first "must survive vs. may shrink"
classification was introduced for every element, and legibility floors
were set for elements permitted to shrink.

**Permanent rule:** Every ad must be evaluated at realistic mobile-feed
scale, not only at full canvas size — legibility at that scale is a
first-class design requirement, not an afterthought check.

**Category:** Mobile Readability.

## FL-021 — Dark background theme

**Campaign:** Early Halliburton/Bilfinger passes.

**What failed:** A dark, black-background "premium poster" theme was used
in place of the category's actual white-newsprint convention.

**Why it failed commercially:** Scored low with the client feedback that
it read as a design mockup, not a real recruitment classified — alien to
the medium candidates actually trust, and it discards the print-newspaper
credibility signal entirely.

**Root cause:** "Premium" was interpreted as a generic dark, moody visual
style rather than being grounded in the actual conventions of the target
publication (*Assignments Abroad Times*).

**How it was discovered:** Direct client scoring (2/10) and explicit
feedback: "you are creating documents not ads… white newsprint."

**How it was corrected:** Rebuilt on white newsprint with black body text
and, where used, spot-colour accents — never a dark or black canvas.

**Permanent rule:** Overseas-recruitment classifieds are never a dark
background. White page, black ink (with optional spot-colour accents) is
the entire credible design space for this category.

**Category:** Visual Story / Branding.

## FL-022 — Fabricated / scraped logo

**Campaign:** Travel Gate-related pass.

**What failed:** A client logo was scraped/cropped from a photograph
(a bus image) and used as if it were a supplied brand asset.

**Why it failed commercially:** A distorted, low-quality, non-authorized
reproduction of a brand mark damages both the client's brand and the
agency's professional credibility — worse than omitting the logo
entirely.

**Root cause:** The design process treated "some visual representation of
the brand" as an acceptable substitute for an actual supplied logo file.

**How it was discovered:** Direct client correction: "if client provide
no logo don't add logo, just deliver what you have."

**How it was corrected:** Logo usage restricted to real, client-supplied
files only; when none is supplied, the space is given to a plain text
header instead.

**Permanent rule:** Never scrape, crop, trace, or typeset a substitute for
a logo. Use exactly what is supplied, or omit it — a missing logo is
acceptable, a fabricated one is not.

**Category:** Truth / Branding / Trust.

## FL-023 — Wrong currency risk (unresolved destination)

**Campaign:** Cross-campaign, Currency Intelligence design.

**What failed:** Risk of a salary figure being rendered with a currency
symbol that does not match the destination country, or an unrecognized
destination receiving a guessed currency.

**Why it failed commercially:** A currency mismatch is not stylistic — it
is a factual error that could mislead a candidate about real earnings,
directly violating the category's basic trust contract.

**Root cause:** Currency could be inferred loosely from context rather
than being strictly derived from a fixed destination-country lookup.

**How it was discovered:** Identified proactively during Country/Currency
Intelligence design and locked as a hard validation gate before any
campaign could ship with the risk live.

**How it was corrected:** Currency is derived strictly from a fixed
GCC destination table (Saudi Arabia→SAR, UAE→AED, Kuwait→KWD, Qatar→QAR,
Bahrain→BHD, Oman→OMR); an unresolved destination invalidates the ad
rather than defaulting to a guess.

**Permanent rule:** Currency is never inferred loosely — it is looked up
from a fixed destination table, and an unresolved destination is a hard
failure, never a best-guess default.

**Category:** Truth.

## FL-024 — Single dominant message split across two competing claims

**Campaign:** General risk class, guarded against before shipping.

**What failed:** Risk of an ad simultaneously pushing two equally loud
claims (e.g. "MEGA PROJECT" and "PREMIUM EMPLOYER" and "URGENT HIRING" all
as co-equal leads).

**Why it failed commercially:** A reader's attention has one entry point;
two equally weighted claims split it and both under-deliver, producing a
busier ad that persuades less than a single-focused one.

**Root cause:** No ranking step existed to force a single winner among the
available persuasive levers (country, salary, industry, project,
employer, positions, benefits, interview, trust).

**How it was discovered:** Identified as a structural risk during the V12
review process, generalized from the specific "no dominant hook" finding
(FL-002, FL-018).

**How it was corrected:** Every ad now ranks its available levers and
commits to exactly one dominant hero; all other true facts remain visible
but subordinate.

**Permanent rule:** Rank every true, available selling lever and commit to
exactly one as the dominant hero. Never let two facts compete for the
same visual weight at the top of the ad.

**Category:** Psychology / Hierarchy.

## FL-025 — Content truncation losing grounded facts

**Campaign:** Dense position-list layouts.

**What failed:** Long position titles were cut with an ellipsis when they
didn't fit their assigned row width.

**Why it failed commercially:** A truncated job title loses the exact
fact a candidate is scanning for — an information-loss failure, not just
a cosmetic one.

**Root cause:** Row layout used a fixed font size regardless of the
longest title that needed to fit.

**How it was discovered:** Direct visual inspection of a rendered dense
position table.

**How it was corrected:** Row font size is reduced until every position
title fits in full on one line; text is never shortened or ellipsized.

**Permanent rule:** When content and space conflict, shrink the type —
never cut, abbreviate, or truncate a grounded fact.

**Category:** Truth / Hierarchy.

## FL-026 — Ungrouped dense position list

**Campaign:** 129-position multi-vacancy requirement.

**What failed:** A large position count was laid out as one flat,
undifferentiated list.

**Why it failed commercially:** A wall of ungrouped job titles is
unscannable — a candidate cannot quickly find their own trade/division,
so the ad fails its core job of self-selection at scale.

**Root cause:** Position layout logic did not group roles by discipline
once the count grew large.

**How it was discovered:** Direct visual inspection of a rendered layout
for a 129-position requirement.

**How it was corrected:** Positions above a size threshold are grouped
into a multi-column layout of division/discipline cards, each internally
ordered.

**Permanent rule:** Any position list beyond roughly a dozen roles must be
grouped by discipline/division — never rendered as one undifferentiated
list.

**Category:** Hierarchy / Candidate Behaviour.

## FL-027 — Garish default colour choices

**Campaign:** Early agency-identity treatments.

**What failed:** Colour defaults leaned on raw primary yellow and red
rather than a curated, premium palette.

**Why it failed commercially:** Garish primary colours read as cheap or
discount-oriented rather than premium, undercutting trust in a category
where the agency's credibility (licence, registration) is the core trust
anchor.

**Root cause:** Colour selection had no destination-aware, curated palette
to draw from, so it defaulted to raw, uncurated primaries.

**How it was discovered:** Direct client feedback: "can you come out of
yellow and red deep… see other colours also can be used which looks
perfect in classified."

**How it was corrected:** A curated, destination-aware palette (desert
gold, deep blue, burgundy, cream + dark + gold, blue + gold) replaced raw
primary defaults.

**Permanent rule:** Never default to raw primary yellow/red. Colour is
chosen from a curated, destination-aware premium palette.

**Category:** Colour / Branding.

## FL-028 — Plain, undifferentiated agency-name treatment

**Campaign:** Cross-campaign, footer/header identity.

**What failed:** The agency name was rendered as plain text with no
distinct typographic or visual treatment of its own.

**Why it failed commercially:** A flat agency-name treatment undersells
the one brand element the agency actually owns outright across every
campaign it runs.

**Root cause:** Typography rules specified hierarchy for facts (headline,
positions, salary) but did not assign the agency name its own dedicated
treatment.

**How it was discovered:** Direct client feedback that the agency name
"not attractive" needed distinct treatment.

**How it was corrected:** The agency name was given deliberate,
consistent typographic treatment distinct from plain body text, applied
across every campaign the agency runs.

**Permanent rule:** The agency name always receives dedicated visual
treatment — it is the one piece of brand equity carried across every
campaign and must never be rendered as plain, undifferentiated text.

**Category:** Branding.

## FL-029 — Inconsistent, per-campaign font choices

**Campaign:** Early cross-campaign experimentation.

**What failed:** Fonts were chosen ad hoc per campaign rather than from a
fixed, shared system.

**Why it failed commercially:** Inconsistent typography reads as amateur
production and makes it harder for a candidate who sees multiple KAI ads
to recognize and trust a single, professional publisher across
campaigns.

**Root cause:** No locked, role-based typography system existed; each ad
made its own font decisions.

**How it was discovered:** Identified while auditing type usage across
Halliburton, Bilfinger, and Bus Driver passes for consistency.

**How it was corrected:** Locked to a five-role system (hero/company
name, section bars/headers, dense position lists, salary/emphasis,
address/fine print), applied identically across every campaign.

**Permanent rule:** Typography is a fixed five-role hierarchy, not a
per-campaign creative choice — every element maps to exactly one role
across every campaign.

**Category:** Typography / Branding.

## FL-030 — Benchmark contamination from a single reference agency

**Campaign:** Framework-design stage (pre-campaign).

**What failed:** Early framing treated one real newspaper agency's box as
the thing to imitate directly, risking that agency's specific data and
habits being baked into the general design system.

**Why it failed commercially:** A design system that quietly encodes one
tenant's habits will misfit every other tenant and client using the same
system — it stops being generic infrastructure.

**Root cause:** No explicit separation existed between "grammar research"
(how DTP classifieds are generally built) and "acceptance benchmark"
(what KAI's own system is judged against).

**How it was discovered:** Identified and corrected proactively during
framework design, before it could ship as a live defect: "benchmark must
be our own framework," not any specific agency or client.

**How it was corrected:** The real paper was kept strictly as grammar
research; the acceptance benchmark was locked as KAI's own rule set,
applied generically to whichever tenant/client data is supplied.

**Permanent rule:** Study real classifieds for grammar only, never for
tenant-specific content. The acceptance bar is always KAI's own rule set,
applied generically.

**Category:** Trust / Candidate Behaviour.

## Cross-cutting observation

Every failure above resolves to one of two underlying causes: (1) a
**visual/creative decision made without a ranking or protection rule**
(what leads, what's protected minimum size, what colour is committed to),
or (2) a **fact handled carelessly** (truncated, fabricated, misclassified,
or mismatched to its real source). The Creative Director Brain exists
specifically to make (1) and (2) structural guarantees rather than
per-campaign judgment calls.
